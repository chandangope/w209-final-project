Home and rental price market analysis

Introduction

Experts believe the U.S. housing market is hot. Well not necessarily in a good way for everyone - rising home values and rental prices may mean less affordability for the general population. Shiller home price index shows a relentless rise. Where does it stop, is the question many are asking.

Booming housing market nevertheless, offers plenty of opportunities for investors. A data driven approach would enable them to figure out the best places to invest in, based on various factors they might want to consider. Our team collected some housing market data from Zillow and created some visualizations with the goal of enabling these investors make an objective decision.

Audience

The intended audience for our visualizations primarily include capital investors and individuals, who are looking into buying real estate for investment purposes. We have also catered to people who might be considering renting vs buying for their own dwelling.

Data

Our primary source of data has been Zillow. The data is organized into geographic levels of state, metro, county, zipcode and neighborhood. For each geographic cut, we have several (80+) csv files, each keeping information on a specific measure for e.g. median listing price, median rental price, inventory measure, days on Zillow, etc. This dataset from Zillow does not provide information on population measures, and since population dynamics influences the demand and inventory levels significantly, we augmented our data with population measures (US census) also.

Insights

Our visualizations offer both - a high level overview of the markets as well as the capability to drill down deeper. We first summarize some high level insights, followed by a case study

  • Overall the US housing and rental market is indeed very hot.
  • It is expected that the trend will continue at least for the next year.
  • Nevada seems to standout in terms of population and rental growth rates.
  • Along the west and east coasts, the breakeven time is approximately 4 years, which is higher by couple of years compared to most other parts of the country.
  • With a monthly mortgage payment capability of $500, there is almost nowhere in the Western half of the country where you can afford a house, while it is possible in other parts of the country
  • June-July is the hottest period for the housing market

Case study of San Francisco bay area

Most places in the SFO bay area have returned 8% Compounded annual returns since 2011! Homes with values over 1 million are expected to give the lowest returns of upto 3%, whereas lower valued areas as Fremont, Oakland, Berkeley, Hayward all are expected to return anywhere from 7 to 20%. While compounded returns may not look rosy, the breakeven chart highlights rapid rental cost escalation with regions in the bay becoming overheated within 3 years even with million $ median home values. Still, one needs atleast 3500$ a month to be able to afford mortgage payments in the eastern bay area. In South SF, Santa clara region, one would need 5000$+. Home sold in Jan take 90 days to sell, while those in May sell in 2 months, not offering enough bargaining room. From an investment perspective, eastern bay cities such as Fremont, Pleasanton, Oakland offer the best return on investment with double digit growths. Central Bay area specifically Santa Clara county have all risen by double digits until now, and is expected to cool down. The central few zip codes in Redwood city may offer reasonable returns with relatively lower buying prices.
